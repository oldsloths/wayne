Ben-Hur and the Duke / Hollywood heroics in Charlton Heston's memoir and a John Wayne biography

Mick LaSalle
Oct. 22, 1995Updated: Feb. 4, 2012 7:19 a.m.
Facebook Twitter Email

JOHN WAYNE: AMERICAN

By Randy Roberts and James S. Olson

Free Press; 738 pages, $27.50

IN THE ARENA

By Charlton Heston

Simon & Schuster; 592 pages; $27.50

John Wayne and Charlton Heston are rugged heroes of the screen, best known for playing strong, stoic men from an earlier time. They have something else in common -- conservative politics, which has often colored people's perception of them, for good or ill.

Their lives are profiled in two new books: "John Wayne: American," by history Professors Randy Roberts and James S. Olson; and "In the Arena," Heston's autobiography. These are long, surprisingly entertaining books about two talented, frequently underrated stars.

Of the two books, "John Wayne: American" is the more flawed but fascinating, perhaps because Wayne himself was such a strange, contradictory, enigmatic figure. Charming yet surly and resentful. A great pal -- and a lousy husband. A flag-waving patriot, and a man who, unique among the major film actors of his generation, apparently did everything he could to avoid any kind of military service.

The authors try to present Wayne as the embodiment, on screen and off, of the kind of no-frills nobility some like to see as distinctly American. But they have a hard time justifying that view in light of their own evidence. A typical sentence: "In an age when most leading men cultivated East Coast or European accents, John Wayne spoke with a voice that was purely American." If an East Coast accent isn't purely American, what is it?

At its worst, "John Wayne: American" reads like a book with a grudge to settle. The authors are annoyed with some urban, left-of-center film critics who have discounted Wayne's achievement and talent. But instead of countering this neglect by demonstrating that Wayne was a great star and a unique, formidable talent, irrespective of politics, the authors commit the opposite absurdity, claiming that the chord Wayne struck with audiences was a function of his political views: "It would take the political firestorm of the 1960s to . . . elevate Duke from star to icon. . . . Like the defenders of the Alamo in 1836, he drew a line in the sand and stood his political ground."

The screen image of Wayne is so tantalizing that the authors often get confused, attributing to him the qualities of moral and physical courage evident only in his two bouts with cancer. Wayne himself often seemed to get confused, saying at one point of a senator who criticized him, "I wish this were the 1800s. I'd horsewhip him."

Despite its faults and biases, "John Wayne: American" is well-written and meticulously researched. Touching on the making of many of Wayne's films, it explains how the Wayne persona developed gradually over the course of the 65 B-movies made during the 1930s. It tells of the actor's conscious creation of the Wayne walk (the toes hit the ground first) and the Wayne mode of speech (pauses come between sentences).

The chapters on Wayne's conservative activism in the '60s are intriguing. While other stars who spoke in favor of the Vietnam War had had combat experience -- for example, Brigadier General James Stewart and Lieutenant Commander Robert Montgomery -- Wayne had spent the World War II years drinking heavily, cheating on his first wife and advancing his career. It's hard not to read guilt as a factor behind his stridency a generation later.

To the last page, Wayne holds up as an arresting, likable yet often dark figure -- far from the hero the authors would have us believe but something far more complicated and effete: an artist.

In contrast to Wayne, Heston in "In the Arena" comes across as a man who's onto himself, who knows his strengths and limitations and has a sense of humor. At times the book rambles, but it has the charm of an autobiography. Heston's voice is there, soothing, avuncular, slightly reserved.

Heston and the reader have the most fun in the early sections of the book, in which Heston writes of his college courtship of his wife, Lydia, to whom he's been married more than 50 years. Heston was one of those G.I.s who got married by a justice of the peace just before entering World War II.

It's always interesting to read of an actor's struggle from obscurity to superstardom. In his early years trying to crack the New York stage, Heston had his share of frustrations. During his first month in New York he tells of being cast as Mercutio in a Broadway production of "Romeo and Juliet," in which the Montagues were white and the Capulets were black. Unfortunately the show's backing fell through. His first break came a year later -- playing a minor role in a production of "Antony and Cleopatra" starring Katherine Cornell.

"In the Arena" is rich in anecdotes about some of the biggest names in entertainment and politics over the past 50 years. In one he explains that Orson Welles was asked by a young fan how, in "Citizen Kane," anyone knew Kane's last words, since Kane died with no one else in the room. "Orson looked at him a moment," Heston writes, "then put a massive hand on the back of his neck and drew him close. 'You must never,' he rumbled softly, 'repeat one word of what you have just told me to a living soul.' "

Throughout Heston gives tips on the craft of film acting, discussing, for example, how to play a passionate love scene while remembering not to block the leading lady's chin with your shoulder or lean into her key light.

On the political front Heston's conservatism comes across as intelligent, considered and consistent, unlike Wayne's, which was sentimental, paranoid and spiteful. More a classic libertarian than a right- winger, Heston marched with Martin Luther King in the early '60s, then drifted rightward in the late '60s and '70s.

Though Heston gets cranky on the subject of "political correctness," his main interest is acting. The past 45 years of his life, as described in "In the Arena," are a catalog of film and stage appearances. The details surrounding some of these are more interesting than others.

Heston seems to understand that and rushes through the '70s and '80s, while devoting pages to what it was like to drive a chariot in "Ben-Hur." id

Mick LaSalle is the film critic for the San Francisco Chronicle, where he has worked since 1985. He is the author of two books on pre-censorship Hollywood, "Complicated Women: Sex and Power in Pre-Code Hollywood" and "Dangerous Men: Pre-Code Hollywood and the Birth of the Modern Man." Both were books of the month on Turner Classic Movies and "Complicated Women" formed the basis of a TCM documentary in 2003, narrated by Jane Fonda. He has written introductions for a number of books, including Peter Cowie's "Joan Crawford: The Enduring Star" (2009). He was a panelist at the Berlin Film Festival and has served as a panelist for eight of the last ten years at the Venice Film Festival.  His latest book, a study of women in French cinema, is "The Beauty of the Real: What Hollywood Can Learn from Contemporary French Actresses."
